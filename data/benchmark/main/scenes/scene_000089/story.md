# scene_000089

A wildlife veterinarian in a rural Appalachian clinic documented three critical observations while examining a patient who had sustained a bite from a feral raccoon the previous week. The medical record clearly indicated **Contact with Rabid Animal** marked as "yes," reflecting the confirmed exposure through broken skin that introduced saliva-based viral particles. When laboratory tests returned from the state health department, they confirmed **Rabies Virus Infection** as "positive," establishing that the lyssavirus had successfully replicated and infiltrated the central nervous system following the original traumatic inoculation.

As the encephalitic syndrome progressed over the following seventy-two hours, the clinical picture evolved into its characteristic neurological presentation. The patient developed the pathognomonic manifestation documented in the chart as **Hydrophobia Symptom** marked "present," experiencing violent pharyngeal spasms and autonomic panic responses whenever presented with water to drink. This inability to swallow liquids served as definitive evidence that the active viral infection had advanced to the brainstem, completing the predictable clinical trajectory from initial exposure through confirmed systemic infection to the terrifying neurological complications that define end-stage disease.
