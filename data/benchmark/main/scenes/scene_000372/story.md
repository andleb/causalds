# scene_000372

When a patient presents with severe bacterial pneumonia, clinicians first quantify the Airway Bacterial Load, typically measuring pathogen concentration in log10 CFU/ml from sputum or bronchoscopic samples. This quantitative measure serves as the critical starting point for understanding disease severity. As the bacterial burden increases, these organisms directly invade alveolar spaces, producing fibrin-rich exudates and consolidation that expand the Radiographic Infiltrate Volume, measured in cubic centimeters on computed tomography scans. The mechanical spread of infection through lung parenchyma creates direct tissue damage independent of systemic complications.

However, as the Airway Bacterial Load continues to rise, transcending local containment, the binary transition to Systemic Sepsis occurs (coded as 1 for presence, 0 for absence) when bacteria translocate into the bloodstream, triggering a cytokine cascade. Once Systemic Sepsis develops, it independently amplifies lung injury beyond the direct infectious damage, driving the Radiographic Infiltrate Volume to expand further through inflammatory edema and acute respiratory distress mechanisms. Thus, the final Radiographic Infiltrate Volume reflects not merely the local bacterial burden measured in log10 CFU/ml, but also the compounded injury from both the direct infection and the systemic inflammatory response, while the binary status of Systemic Sepsis remains determined by the initial bacterial invasion.
