As meteorologists monitored the developing tropical cyclone, they recorded the Sea Surface Temperature at 29 degrees Celsius in the pre-storm oceanic conditions, which thermodynamically fueled the system to reach a Cyclone Maximum Wind Speed of 180 km/h at landfall. Both the elevated thermal energy provided by the warm ocean waters and the intense rotational dynamics of the storm itself contributed to substantial atmospheric moisture convergence, resulting in Total Precipitation exceeding 300 mm across the interior coastal plain. The powerful Cyclone Maximum Wind Speed generated significant onshore momentum transfer and frictional water displacement against the shoreline, causing Significant Storm Surge Occurred to register as 'yes' along the barrier island communities. In the post-event disaster assessment, epidemiologists documented 45 Fatalities due to Wind and Storm Surge Only, determining that these deaths resulted specifically from the combined destructive forces of the high-velocity aerial assault and the coastal inundation events, explicitly excluding any casualties attributed to inland freshwater flooding despite the substantial Total Precipitation accumulation recorded elsewhere in the region.
