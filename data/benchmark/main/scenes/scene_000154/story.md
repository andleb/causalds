# scene_000154

Farmers in the semi-arid region receive an Irrigation Activation Subsidy, measured in continuous dollars per hectare, which creates a direct economic incentive affecting whether an Irrigation Event is Executed (0/1 binary) on their cropland. However, this irrigation decision also critically depends on the Subsurface Soil Permeability-an unobserved continuous property measured in cm/hour that remains latent to agricultural economists but governs how water infiltrates the soil profile. When subsidy levels are sufficient to offset costs and the underlying soil structure allows adequate infiltration, the binary Irrigation Event Executed switches to 1, activating water delivery systems; conversely, insufficient subsidies or impermeable subsoils result in a 0 (non-execution).

The Field Soil Erosion Rate, recorded continuously in tons per hectare per year, emerges as the ultimate outcome influenced by this causal chain. When an Irrigation Event is Executed (1), the mechanical application of water and subsequent flow patterns directly disturb the topsoil, contributing to measurable erosion increases on that field. Simultaneously, the Subsurface Soil Permeability exerts its own direct effect on erosion dynamics-higher permeability typically facilitates water infiltration and minimizes surface runoff, thereby reducing soil loss, while lower permeability promotes runoff that intensifies topsoil displacement. Thus, the observed erosion rate reflects the combined action of the binary irrigation intervention and the continuous, unobserved soil hydraulic properties working in concert.
