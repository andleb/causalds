# scene_000425

In a controlled climate chamber, researchers exposed white oak samples to varying Ambient Relative Humidity levels, cycling between dry 35% and humid 90% conditions over several weeks. When the chamber maintained 90% Ambient Relative Humidity, the wood equilibrated until its internal moisture content surpassed the 30% threshold, registering as Wood Moisture Above Fiber Saturation Point = 1, indicating that cell walls were fully saturated with bound water and free water had begun accumulating within the cellular lumens. During this saturated phase, despite the persistently high Ambient Relative Humidity, the Dimensional Change in Wood measured only 0.1 mm across the tangential plane, as the fully saturated cell walls could absorb no additional bound water to trigger expansion. However, when Ambient Relative Humidity dropped to 35%, the internal moisture fell below the critical saturation threshold, switching Wood Moisture Above Fiber Saturation Point to 0, and the combination of this below-saturation state acting alongside the direct desorptive pressure from the dry Ambient Relative Humidity drove significant contraction, producing a Dimensional Change in Wood of 2.8 mm as the cell walls released bound water and shrank.
