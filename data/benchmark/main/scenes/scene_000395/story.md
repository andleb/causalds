# scene_000395

In the sweltering summer of 1854, Dr. Edmund Hargrove conducted systematic surveys through London's Broad Street neighborhood, meticulously recording the Sewage Odor Intensity wafting from alleyways and cesspools using a standardized olfactory index. Where decomposing organic matter festered in poorly drained courtyards, his measurements registered olfactory index values exceeding 8.5, producing the characteristic 'miasma' that prevailing medical theory held responsible for epidemic disease. The noxious vapors seemed to permeate the very brickwork of the tenements, creating an invisible poison that physicians believed could spontaneously generate Cholera Infection Status among susceptible residents.

Yet Hargrove observed a secondary phenomenon proliferating in direct proportion to the fetid air: Housefly Population Density reached extraordinary concentrations of over 150 flies per cubic meter in the same courtyards where sewage odors peaked. The insects bred furiously in the moist filth, their numbers increasing steadily as the Sewage Odor Intensity climbed, creating a measurable gradient of infestation that paralleled the stench. Public health inspectors documented these mechanical vectors swarming over exposed food and open wounds, suspecting their role in spreading contagion while simultaneously attributing primary causation to the corrupt atmosphere itself.

When analyzing the outbreak data, Hargrove discovered that Cholera Infection Status-coded as 1 for afflicted patients and 0 for those spared-correlated both with high olfactory index measurements and with extreme Housefly Population Density counts. Households exposed to Sewage Odor Intensity above 7.0 showed elevated infection rates even when fly populations were controlled, supporting the miasma theory's direct causal claim. Simultaneously, however, clusters where Housefly Population Density exceeded 120 flies per cubic meter exhibited additional infection risk independent of odor levels, suggesting these insects served as active transmission agents rather than merely indicators of unsanitary conditions. The dual pathway of disease-through vapor inhalation and insect-borne contamination-created a devastating convergence of factors that devastated the district before the true waterborne nature of the epidemic was understood.
