# scene_000014

When a patient arrives at the triage desk, the clinician must weigh two critical but distinct factors to determine whether Hospital Admission is warranted. First, there is the Distance to Nearest Hospital-measured in kilometers from the patient's residence-which creates practical constraints on outpatient management; patients residing 40 kilometers away may be admitted for observation even when borderline cases from nearby areas might be sent home. Second, there is the underlying Injury Severity, an unmeasured physiological burden scored on a 0-10 scale that influences clinical judgment: more severe trauma virtually guarantees admission regardless of geography, while minor injuries may result in admission only when compounded by significant travel barriers. Once the binary decision to admit is made, the Length of Hospital Stay in days becomes determined by two separate forces. The Injury Severity directly drives resource utilization, as higher severity scores on that latent 0-10 scale require extended monitoring, surgical interventions, and rehabilitation regardless of admission logistics. Simultaneously, the Hospital Admission itself initiates the clock on institutional care-the act of being admitted creates the pathway for length of stay to accumulate, transforming the severity-driven medical necessity into measured days of hospitalization.
