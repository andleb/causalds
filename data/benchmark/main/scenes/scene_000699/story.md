# scene_000699

In a controlled clinical trial examining exertional heat stress, researchers monitored elite athletes under two distinct pharmacological protocols. Some participants received Aspirin Administration coded as 1, while control subjects remained at 0, receiving no antiplatelet medication. Each athlete then performed standardized treadmill running at 75% VO₂ max, with the Duration of Strenuous Exercise carefully recorded in continuous minutes, ranging from brief 20-minute sessions to prolonged exertion lasting 90 minutes. Throughout the protocol, medical staff continuously monitored each participant's Core Body Temperature in degrees Celsius using gastrointestinal thermistors to track the precise development of hyperthermia during activity.

The physiological data revealed that Core Body Temperature rose in direct response to prolonged physical exertion, with athletes completing 90 minutes of activity showing measurably higher temperatures than those completing only 20-minute sessions. Meanwhile, the pharmacological intervention independently influenced thermoregulatory outcomes; Aspirin Administration altered hypothalamic temperature setpoints and affected the body's heat dissipation mechanisms during exercise stress. Consequently, the final Core Body Temperature readings reflected the combined physiological influence of both independent factors-the administered aspirin and the exertional duration-with neither variable showing any direct dependence on or manipulation of the other in this experimental design.
