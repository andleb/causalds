# scene_000414

Dr. Sarah Chen evaluated a 58-year-old patient who had been living with uncontrolled type 2 diabetes for over a decade, confirming his Diabetes Mellitus Status as 1 on the clinical assessment scale. The prolonged exposure to elevated blood glucose levels had progressively damaged the microvasculature supplying his peripheral nerves, leading to metabolic changes within the nerve fibers themselves. When Sarah reviewed his diagnostic results, she documented that Peripheral Nerve Injury was definitively present-yes-with evidence of both demyelination and axonal degeneration characteristic of advanced diabetic neuropathy. This structural damage to the sensory fibers had lowered their activation thresholds and upregulated sodium channel expression, fundamentally altering how his nervous system processed sensory input.

During his physical examination, the patient winced visibly when Sarah lightly brushed a cotton wisp against his feet, demonstrating clear mechanical allodynia. She recorded that Allodynia was present in his clinical chart, noting how this paradoxical symptom transformed innocuous stimuli like bedsheets or clothing contact into sharp, burning pain sensations. The peripheral sensitization resulting from his nerve damage had caused his mechanoreceptors to activate pain pathways in response to stimuli that should never have triggered discomfort, creating a daily burden where even standing or wearing socks became agonizing experiences.
