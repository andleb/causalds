# scene_000504

In the highland villages of Oaxaca, Mexico, communities situated at extreme Geographic Remoteness from Urban Centers-often exceeding 120 kilometers from the nearest paved road or clinic-experience a unique intersection of infectious disease burden and supernatural explanatory models. This isolation creates conditions where Community Subclinical Parasite Burden, measured at levels averaging 850 eggs per gram of feces among asymptomatic carriers, drives Population Hookworm Prevalence to nearly 34 percent and Population Ascariasis Prevalence to approximately 28 percent throughout the region. Concurrently, the same Geographic Remoteness from Urban Centers fosters traditional belief systems where illness is attributed to supernatural causation, resulting in community members rating their Perceived Curse Severity at an average of 7.3 on a standardized 0-10 scale when experiencing unexplained symptoms.

When individuals perceive themselves as targets of malevolent supernatural forces, indicated by high scores on the Perceived Curse Severity scale, they develop significant Curse-Related Psychological Distress, with anxiety scores frequently reaching 16 or higher on the 0-21 validated instrument. This psychological burden manifests physically, as both the intensity of the Perceived Curse Severity and the subsequent Curse-Related Psychological Distress independently contribute to clinical presentations meeting criteria for Somatic Symptom Disorder Diagnosis (coded as 1 for positive diagnosis). Medical anthropologists working in these regions note that the binary diagnosis of Somatic Symptom Disorder often emerges not from organic pathology alone, but from the potent psychosomatic interplay between supernatural beliefs and physiological dysregulation, while the underlying Population Ascariasis Prevalence and Population Hookworm Prevalence continue to reflect the persistent environmental risks associated with extreme Geographic Remoteness from Urban Centers.
