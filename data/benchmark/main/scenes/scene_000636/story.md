# scene_000636

During the post-storm hydrological assessment, meteorologists recorded the Storm Precipitation Volume at 215 mm across the watershed over a twelve-hour period. This intense accumulation interacted with the terrain's intrinsic Soil Permeability Index-which remained unobserved by field instruments but manifested as a severely limited infiltration capacity of approximately 1.5 mm/hour due to antecedent saturation and clay-heavy subsoils-generating a Peak Flood Depth of 2.8 meters in the residential quarter. The flood measurements were continuously distributed, ranging from shallow inundation of 0.4 meters in peripheral zones to the maximum depth approaching 3 meters in topographic depressions.

The engineering assessment revealed that the Peak Flood Depth directly precipitated structural damage as hydrostatic pressure breached ground-floor systems and compromised electrical infrastructure. However, the Soil Permeability Index exerted an additional independent influence on building integrity through prolonged subsurface saturation and foundation soil weakening, ensuring that Severe Damage Occurrence-coded as a strict binary outcome where a value of 1 indicated catastrophic structural failure and 0 indicated recoverable conditions-afflicted properties even in zones with moderate flood levels. Thus, the unobserved soil characteristics revealed their causal impact through both the amplification of surface flooding and the direct degradation of substructure stability.
