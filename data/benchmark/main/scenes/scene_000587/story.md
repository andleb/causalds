# scene_000587

In a large occupational health cohort study, researchers scheduled workers for baseline evaluations at different times, recording the Time of Day of Baseline Stress Assessment in hours on a 24-hour clock. Because cortisol follows a strong circadian rhythm-typically peaking shortly after awakening and declining throughout the day-workers assessed later in the evening often exhibited different physiological profiles than those tested in the morning. This measurement timing directly influenced whether individuals were classified with High Occupational Stress Status, as elevated evening cortisol or fatigue-related responses biased the binary stress classification.

However, the study also recognized that an Unmeasured Genetic Predisposition to Cardiovascular Disease, quantified as a continuous liability score aggregating thousands of small-effect variants, independently shaped both stress physiology and vascular outcomes. This latent genetic factor contributed to High Occupational Stress Status by influencing individual stress reactivity, autonomic nervous system responses, and potentially occupational selection into high-demand roles. Simultaneously, this same Unmeasured Genetic Predisposition to Cardiovascular Disease directly elevated the risk of an Incident Ischemic Stroke Event over the follow-up period, representing the inherited thrombotic and atherosclerotic vulnerabilities that operate regardless of external pressures.

Beyond these background influences, the classification of High Occupational Stress Status itself acted as a distinct pathway to cerebrovascular events. Workers categorized with High Occupational Stress Status showed significantly higher incidence of an Incident Ischemic Stroke Event during the five-year surveillance, as chronic activation of stress-response systems promoted hypertension, inflammation, and endothelial dysfunction. Thus, the observed stroke events emerged from the interplay of measurement timing affecting stress classification, inherited liability conferring baseline vulnerability, and the direct pathophysiological consequences of sustained occupational stress.
