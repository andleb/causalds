# scene_000974

During the late eighteenth century in northern Honshu, domains varied significantly in their **Chronic Regional Poverty Level**, a persistent structural condition rooted in soil quality and tax burdens that rarely appeared in official censuses but fundamentally constrained peasant households. When the **Severe Drought Event** of the Tenmei era arrived-brought on by cooling temperatures and failed monsoons that parched rice paddies-these two independent forces intersected catastrophically. The underlying **Chronic Regional Poverty Level** left communities without surplus grain stores to buffer against harvest failures, while the **Severe Drought Event** destroyed the current year's crop, and together they generated the devastating **Famine Occurrence** that persisted from 1782 to 1788.

The **Famine Occurrence** dramatically intensified survival pressures, but households did not react solely to immediate starvation. The **Chronic Regional Poverty Level** had already established cultural patterns of family limitation among impoverished peasants who viewed additional children as unaffordable luxuries even before the crisis. As the **Famine Occurrence** progressed and food prices became prohibitive, communities turned to **Induced Abortion Practice**-a form of fertility control known among rural populations as a household survival strategy. Thus, **Induced Abortion Practice** emerged from the combined pressure of the acute **Famine Occurrence** and the enduring **Chronic Regional Poverty Level**, while the **Severe Drought Event** acted solely as the external environmental catalyst that initiated the food crisis without directly influencing reproductive decisions except through its role in causing famine.
