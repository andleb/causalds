# scene_000867

In a reproductive endocrinology clinic, patients present with varying degrees of androgen excess that clinicians assess through comprehensive clinical indices, though these baseline severity scores remain as underlying disease burdens not explicitly quantified in administrative records. A 28-year-old patient with moderate hirsutism and elevated biochemical markers faces a decision influenced by two independent factors: her intrinsic baseline hyperandrogenism severity score, which drives both the clinical indication for intervention and her inherent risk of cycle disturbances, and the monthly co-payment for spironolactone set at $45 by her insurance formulary. This out-of-pocket cost, determined externally by pharmacy benefit design rather than clinical factors, directly influences whether she initiates pharmacotherapy alongside her severity profile. When the baseline hyperandrogenism severity score indicates significant biochemical disturbance, clinicians recommend spironolactone treatment, but the patient ultimately fills the prescription only when the monthly co-payment remains within affordable bounds. Consequently, her menstrual irregularity occurrence emerges from the interplay of her underlying endocrine disturbance severity and whether she actually initiated the anti-androgen therapy, with the co-payment acting solely as a barrier to treatment access rather than a direct physiological influence on ovulatory function.
