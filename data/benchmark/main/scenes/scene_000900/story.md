In the aftermath of the 1984 Brighton bombing and years of escalating violence, a latent Political Threshold for Mandate Authorization permeated the bilateral atmosphere between London and Dublin, representing the accumulated political capital and mutual security imperatives that remained largely invisible to public observers but nevertheless determined whether formal diplomacy could proceed. By late 1983, this underlying Political Threshold for Mandate Authorization had reached sufficient intensity to trigger the issuance of a Formal Negotiation Mandate, authorizing Sir Robert Armstrong and his diplomatic counterparts to commence confidential intergovernmental talks. Because the Formal Negotiation Mandate had been granted, the process necessarily generated substantial Intergovernmental Negotiation Duration, spanning nearly twenty-two months of secret deliberations between Chequers and Hillsborough Castle as officials hammered out institutional arrangements. This extensive Intergovernmental Negotiation Duration directly enabled the detailed provisions-including the Intergovernmental Conference mechanism and cross-border security cooperation-that determined the Comprehensiveness of the Anglo-Irish Agreement. However, the ultimate Comprehensiveness of the Anglo-Irish Agreement reflected not merely the time invested in bargaining, but also the depth of commitment encoded in the original Political Threshold for Mandate Authorization, which independently ensured that negotiators possessed the necessary political backing to deviate from existing diplomatic precedents and accept unprecedented Irish government consultation rights over Northern Ireland affairs.
