# scene_000202

In the Western Cape province, epidemiologists observed that individuals with a higher South African Ancestry Proportion-often exceeding 0.8 in Afrikaner descendants-faced a double susceptibility to dermatological distress. This elevated ancestry fraction directly increased the likelihood of carrying the KWE Risk Allele Presence, as the pathogenic variant remained embedded in the gene pool due to a founder effect originally traced to the Oudtshoorn district, where the mutation first took hold generations ago. Simultaneously, those with deeper ancestral roots in this South African subpopulation tended to spend more hours in the region's specific winter climate, accumulating greater Weekly Cold Exposure Duration measured in hours per week below 10°C, whether through occupational outdoor labor or unheated rural dwellings endemic to the area.

When winter descended upon the Karoo, the convergence of genetic predisposition and environmental trigger became clinically apparent. The KWE Risk Allele Presence-inherited in an autosomal dominant pattern-drove epidermal dysfunction independently, compromising the skin's barrier integrity regardless of external conditions. However, the Weekly Cold Exposure Duration acted as a separate aggravating factor, with each additional hour below 10°C intensifying vasoconstriction and keratinocyte damage. Together, these influences determined the patient's Erythema Severity Score on the 0-100 clinical index; those carrying the risk allele who also logged extensive cold hours exhibited the highest severity scores, suffering profound erythema, hyperkeratosis, and cyclical peeling across their palms and soles during the winter months.
