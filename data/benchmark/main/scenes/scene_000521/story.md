During the peak years of operation at the New York immigration station, medical officers meticulously recorded the Infectious Disease Carriage Rate among Immigrants, noting percentages that fluctuated between 2% and 8% of arriving passengers depending on seasonal migration patterns and shipboard conditions. Each confirmed case of trachoma, tuberculosis, or suspected cholera generated mandatory documentation, causing the Quarantine Facility Paperwork Volume to expand proportionally-during high-infection months, clerks processed over 4,000 additional files containing medical inspection certificates, isolation transfer orders, and hearing transcripts for detained individuals. This administrative cascade flowed directly from the prevalence of disease itself; as the percentage of infected arrivals climbed, the count of bureaucratic records accumulated linearly, reflecting the operational burden of extended medical detention and legal processing required for each symptomatic immigrant.

Separately, this same Infectious Disease Carriage Rate among Immigrants determined the health of surrounding port communities. When the carriage rate exceeded 6% during the 1892 cholera pandemic or the 1918 influenza waves, waterfront neighborhoods and tenement districts experienced subsequent transmission chains, with the Local Epidemic Outbreak Frequency documenting up to fifteen annual outbreaks in adjacent city wards compared to baseline years registering merely two or three incidents. The Quarantine Facility Paperwork Volume and the Local Epidemic Outbreak Frequency both emerged independently from this common root cause-the underlying prevalence of infection among newcomers-yet the administrative files themselves neither triggered nor suppressed the spread of disease through the municipality. Rather, the paperwork merely chronicled medical interventions at the island facility while the epidemiological consequences propagated through distinct channels of community exposure, person-to-person contact, and secondary infections within the resident population.
