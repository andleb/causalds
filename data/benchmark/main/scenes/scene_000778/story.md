# scene_000778

A 52-year-old patient presented to the hepatology clinic after six months of consuming High-Dose Niacin Supplementation at 3,000 mg/day, a continuous intake far exceeding the recommended dietary allowance and metabolic clearance capacity. Laboratory assessment revealed the patient had developed Hepatic Niacin Accumulation Exceeding Toxic Threshold, a binary pathological state where hepatic tissue saturation surpassed the organ's ability to process the compound, rendering the accumulation present rather than absent. This threshold condition arose directly from the magnitude and persistence of the niacin dosing regimen.

Clinical evaluation subsequently confirmed Niacin-Induced Liver Injury, evidenced by marked elevations in serum aminotransferases exceeding three times the upper limit of normal, decreased albumin synthesis, and characteristic hepatocellular damage patterns. This injury manifested through two distinct causal mechanisms: the High-Dose Niacin Supplementation exerted direct metabolic stress and cytotoxic effects on hepatocytes independent of tissue saturation levels, while the presence of Hepatic Niacin Accumulation Exceeding Toxic Threshold independently precipitated additional cellular damage through metabolic pathway saturation and the formation of reactive intermediates once storage capacities were exceeded.

The clinical team documented that both the continuous daily exposure to high-dose niacin and the binary status of pathological hepatic accumulation converged to produce the observed liver injury, necessitating immediate discontinuation of the supplementation and implementation of supportive care to address the drug-induced hepatotoxicity.
