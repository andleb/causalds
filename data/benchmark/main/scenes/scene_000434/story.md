In the early months of the Harrison administration, political observers began noticing patterns that would define this presidency's turbulent internal dynamics. The President exhibited a profound Dominant Leadership Disposition, measured through psychometric assessments as a high latent factor score characterized by fearless dominance and assertive control tendencies. This underlying personality architecture manifested in two distinct pathways throughout the executive branch. First, the President's dominant disposition directly generated elevated levels of Conflict-Directed Interpersonal Anger, registering 7.8 on the standardized intensity scale during cabinet meetings, particularly when advisors challenged executive prerogatives. Simultaneously, this same dominant temperament contributed to an increased frequency of Cabinet-Level Power Struggles, quantified as 14 discrete dispute incidents during the first hundred days alone, as department heads resisted unilateral decision-making styles.

However, the structural environment surrounding this administration provided its own independent contribution to executive dysfunction. The Congress, mounting significant Institutional Opposition Resources against the White House agenda, deployed an authority index score of 6.2-reflecting unified opposition party control of key committees, aggressive use of oversight powers, and coordinated legislative obstruction strategies. These substantial opposition resources independently amplified the Cabinet-Level Power Struggles, as cabinet members leveraged congressional support to resist presidential directives, transforming policy disagreements into open administrative warfare. The resulting power struggles then served as a conduit, transmitting institutional friction directly into heightened Conflict-Directed Interpersonal Anger, as measured by escalating interpersonal hostility scores reaching 8.4 on the intensity scale during budget negotiations. Thus, the President's temperamental dominance operated both directly and indirectly-through the mediating mechanisms of cabinet disputes-to produce an administration characterized by acute interpersonal antagonism, while institutional opposition independently exacerbated these conflicts by emboldening cabinet resistance.
