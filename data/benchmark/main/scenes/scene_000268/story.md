# scene_000268

When a patient contracts Mycobacterium leprae, clinicians first confirm their **Leprosy Infection Status**, marking the entry point into the disease trajectory. This chronic bacterial infection exhibits a specific affinity for peripheral nerves, progressively damaging neural tissue and frequently resulting in the **Presence of Clinically Significant Peripheral Neuropathy**, which manifests as sensory loss, nerve thickening, and motor deficits in the extremities. The neuropathy develops as the bacteria invade the myelin and axons, creating the anesthetic skin patches and functional impairments characteristic of advancing disease.

To comprehensively assess patient impairment, healthcare providers assign a **Physical Disability Grade** according to the WHO 0-2 scale. This ordinal classification captures disabilities arising through two distinct mechanisms: direct structural damage stemming from the underlying **Leprosy Infection Status**-such as nasal septum collapse or laryngeal involvement independent of neural pathways-and functional limitations caused by the **Presence of Clinically Significant Peripheral Neuropathy**, which can lead to claw hand deformities, plantar ulcers, or visual impairments from lagophthalmos. A patient receives Grade 0 if no disability is detected, Grade 1 when sensory loss is present without visible deformity, or Grade 2 when visible deformities or severe functional impairments are observed, reflecting the cumulative burden of both the infection's direct tissue effects and its neuropathic complications.
