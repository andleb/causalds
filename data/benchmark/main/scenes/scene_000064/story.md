# scene_000064

During the Zika outbreak, epidemiologists tracked pregnant women with confirmed infection, measuring their Peak Maternal Zika Viral Load in log10 RNA copies per milliliter to quantify the intensity of maternal viremia. These measurements revealed that women exhibiting higher viral concentrations faced increased risks of poor pregnancy outcomes through two distinct pathways. First, elevated Peak Maternal Zika Viral Load directly elevated the risk of Congenital Birth Defects, as intense maternal infection could trigger severe placental inflammation and tissue damage even without establishing persistent fetal infection. Second, higher maternal viral loads substantially increased the likelihood of positive Fetal Zika Infection Status, representing successful transplacental transmission of the virus to the developing fetus. Once the fetus acquired infection, this Fetal Zika Infection Status independently drove the occurrence of Congenital Birth Defects, with infected fetuses showing significantly higher rates of structural anomalies such as microcephaly compared to those who remained uninfected despite maternal viremia.
