# scene_000093

Brother Alaric of Whitmore was a figure shrouded in isolation throughout his life in the Benedictine monastery, his Reclusive and Paranoid Temperament present in every interaction. He refused to take meals in the refectory, instead demanding that food be left at his cell door, and he forbade any monk from entering his quarters during his final illness. This deep-seated suspicion of his brethren directly resulted in an Absence of Deathbed Witnesses-when the bell for Matins rang on that cold November morning in 1187, the brothers found him already stiff and cold in his cot, with no one present to document his final hours or administer last rites.

Because Brother Alaric harbored a Reclusive and Paranoid Temperament throughout his life, modern scholars initially suspected he might have taken his own life or been poisoned by enemies he claimed were plotting against him. Compounding these suspicions, the complete Absence of Deathbed Witnesses meant no testimony exists regarding whether he expired peacefully, cried out in pain, or displayed signs of violence. Consequently, a Historical Controversy Regarding Cause of Death remains disputed among medievalists: some argue he died of natural causes given his advanced age, while others point to his erratic behavior and the suspicious circumstances to suggest suicide or murder, leaving the true nature of his demise an enduring mystery.
